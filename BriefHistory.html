Brief History of Skiing

Prehistoric Skiing

Skiing originated during the last Ice Age, with cave drawings suggesting it was used for movement.

The Sami people are credited with being the first skiers. They primarily skied for hunting and transportation.

The earliest skis, dating back to 8000 years BC, were found in Northern China and were made of wood covered in horsehair.

Modern Skiing

Alpine (downhill) skiing evolved in the 18th century from military competitions in Norway involving skiing and shooting.

Olaf Rye became the first known ski jumper in 1809.

Ski design progressed in the 1800s with the invention of the Cambered Ski and the Telemark Ski, which allowed for carving turns.

The steel edge ski was invented in 1928, improving grip and enabling downhill turns.

Hannes Schneider developed the stem christie turn and parallel turn in the 1920s, revolutionizing skiing techniques.

Skiing became popular worldwide by the 1930s, with Hannes Schneider introducing skiing to Japan.

Downhill skiing was first included in the Winter Olympics in 1936.

The invention of the chair lift in 1936 revolutionized skiing as a recreational activity, allowing multiple downhill runs without the need to climb back up the hill.

Freestyle skiing was recognized as a sport in 1979 and debuted at the 1988 Winter Olympics.

Twin-tipped skis were perfected in the mid-nineties, leading to the development of snow parks in ski resorts.

Ski resorts have evolved to offer a range of amenities and activities beyond skiing, attracting visitors from around the world.
